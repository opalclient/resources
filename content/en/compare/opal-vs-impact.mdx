## Free framework, paid product that keeps shipping

Impact cost nothing beyond an optional donor tier, and while the Impact client itself was closed-source, the ClientAPI framework it was built on is Apache-2.0 and public. That is a genuinely good deal if you want free and do not mind that nobody has touched it in years.

Opal costs $8.99 and is closed-source end to end, but it is still being worked on. It shipped for MC 26.2 in 2026, carries 70 modules across combat, movement, visual, world, and utility, and comes with a launcher instead of a manual tweaker install. You are paying for a client that keeps pace with the current version of the game, not one frozen on a release from years ago.

## Baritone automation vs minigame combat

Impact's headline feature was bundling leijurv's Baritone pathfinding bot directly into the client, giving goal-based navigation, mining, and follow automation for grinding on 2b2t-style anarchy servers. Nothing in Opal's module list resembles that, and it never tried to. If pathfinding automation is what you are after, Impact, or its ClientAPI-based successors, is the actual reference point here, not Opal.

Opal points the other way. Its loud set (flight, scaffold, aggressive combat) is built for BedWars/SkyWars-style minigame fights, and its quieter set is built to hold up across most other servers. It is a combat client, not an automation bot, and it was never trying to be one.

## A client that stalled vs one that is still current

Impact is legacy. ClientAPI was archived read-only in 2019, and the client's own development wound down around 2020, so it stays locked to MC 1.12.2. That is a real cost if you play current Minecraft: no updates, no new-version support, nothing changing. Six years is a long time for a version number to sit still.

Opal tracks the current release, MC 26.2, and is actively maintained in 2026, with a launcher, a scripting layer built on GraalJS, and Discord Rich Presence integration. If you want a client that will still be getting updates next year, that is the side of this comparison to stand on.

## Credit where it is due

Building Impact on top of a separately maintained, Apache-2.0 framework was a smart piece of architecture. ClientAPI gave multiple clients a shared, stackable base, complete with its own event bus, GUI, and command system, and Impact was the client that proved the model and popularized Baritone along with it. That open-framework, closed-client split is worth crediting even where the flagship client itself did not survive.
