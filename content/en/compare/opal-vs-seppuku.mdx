## Free and open source, or $8.99 and closed

If cost is the deciding factor, Seppuku wins outright. It is open source: the code is there to read, build, or fork, and nobody is asking you to pay for it. That is a real, honest advantage, and we are not going to argue around it.

Opal costs $8.99, one-time, no subscription. It is closed source. What that money buys is a client somebody is still actively building: a launcher that logs you in and drops you into a match, a real interface instead of a bare clickgui, and a module set kept current for the Minecraft version people are actually playing on right now.

## Two different servers, two different clients

This is the honest part: Seppuku is built for anarchy servers, where the point is chaos and griefing with effectively no rules. Opal is built around BedWars/SkyWars-style minigame combat, tuned for winning organized fights on most servers rather than surviving lawlessness on one. If you are choosing between these two for the same reason, you are probably choosing wrong. They solve different problems for different players.

Credit where it is due: keeping an anarchy client open source and free for as long as Seppuku has is a genuinely useful thing for that corner of the game, even years after development stopped.

## A legacy client vs one still being pushed forward

Seppuku is marked legacy and built for MC 1.12.2, a version most servers moved past years ago. It still works for players who never left that version, but nothing new is coming; the project stopped in place.

Opal tracks MC 26.2, the current release, on Java 25 via Fabric. It is marked active, meaning it keeps getting attention as Minecraft updates, rather than sitting frozen wherever development happened to stop.

## What is documented about each

To be straight about it, public documentation on Seppuku is thin. What is known: it is open source, a Forge mod for MC 1.12.2, built for anarchy play, and several of its modules were later reused by SalHack, which says something about how much other client developers valued its code even after Seppuku itself went quiet.

Opal's architecture is fully documented: 70 modules split across combat, movement, visual, world, and utility, a NanoVG-based interface, and an embedded GraalJS scripting engine that most clients at this price point do not attempt. That is not a knock on Seppuku, just a difference in how much either client has published about itself.
