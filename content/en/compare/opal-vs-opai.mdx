## Two different bets

Opai and Opal aren't really competing on the same axis. Opai bets on breadth and openness: one client stretched across a wide span of Minecraft versions, with a closed core wrapped in an open extension SDK and a GitHub-topic ecosystem of community add-ons. Opal bets on curation: fewer moving parts, more of them decided for you, wrapped in an interface that looks like it was actually designed.

## Where Opai gets it right

Give credit where it's due. An open SDK sitting on top of a closed core, plus a real GitHub-topic marketplace of extensions, is an unusual thing to see in this space, and version coverage that spans far more of Minecraft's history than most paid clients bother with is a genuine strength. If you want one client that follows you across versions, and you like the idea of community-built add-ons, Opai is doing something worth respecting.

## What Opal does instead

Opal ships the opposite way: a curated 70+ feature set, tuned and switched on before you've touched a settings menu, drawn through a glass interface built on NanoVG. There's no SDK to learn and no extension list to assemble. You log in and it's already the client you wanted, not a starter kit for the client you'll eventually build.

## One client, two jobs

Opal is a hybrid. The loud set, flight, scaffold, aggressive combat, is built for BedWars and SkyWars-style fights. The quiet set, subtle aim, reach, velocity, is built to stay calm on most other servers. You switch by where you're playing, not by which extension you remembered to install first.
