## What Dream is for

Dream is honest about its lane: an external ghost client for legacy Minecraft, driven from a remote web or phone panel instead of an in-game menu, built around screenshare survival on MC 1.7.10 through MC 1.8.9. Its record-your-own autoclicker, which replays your actual click cadence instead of a canned pattern, is a genuinely clever answer to click-pattern detection. If you specifically want phone-controlled play on that version range, that is the tool built for exactly that job, and we are not going to pretend otherwise.

## What Opal is for

Opal is a different tool on purpose. It is not something you drive from a phone between fights, it is a modern, polished in-client overlay for MC 26.2, drawn with NanoVG, running 70 tuned modules you control without ever leaving the game. The point is a finished, good-looking client on current Minecraft, not a legacy control surface you pair before you can play.

## One client, two jobs

Opal is a hybrid. The loud set (flight, scaffold, aggressive combat) is built for BedWars and SkyWars-style fights. The quiet set (subtle aim, reach, velocity) is built to stay calm on most servers. You switch between them from inside the game, no second device required.

## The version gap, and what "setup" means

Dream's whole design center is legacy Minecraft, MC 1.7.10 through MC 1.8.9, tuned for screenshare survival on old-school PvP. If that is your era, the years of focus behind it are real, not a knock against it. But there is nothing here if you play current Minecraft, Opal exists specifically because MC 26.2 needed a client of its own.

Setup tells the same story from a different angle. Dream's path is pairing a remote web or phone panel before you are actually playing. Opal's path is a launcher, a login, and you are in game. A remote control panel is a legitimate feature if that is what you are shopping for, it is just a different kind of shopping than "open the launcher and play."
