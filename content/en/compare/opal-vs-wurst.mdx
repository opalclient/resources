## Free and open source vs $8.99 and finished

Credit where it's due: Wurst costs nothing and the source is sitting right there under GPLv3. Read every line, fork it, ship your own build, never pay a cent. It's been in continuous public release since 2014, longer than almost anything else still standing in this space. That's not a marketing angle, that's just true.

Opal costs $8.99. What that buys is somebody else's finished work instead of a blank toolkit: a custom UI running on NanoVG and a Blaze3D-based effects engine, 70 modules that arrive already tuned instead of waiting on you to tune them, and a launcher that handles login and updates so your first session isn't a setup session. Wurst gives you the parts. Opal gives you the assembled thing.

## Anarchy toolkit vs minigame combat: different jobs

Wurst's own module list tells you exactly what it's for: Tunneller for strip-mining, Tree-bot for autonomous wood farming, AutoFish, dupe-glitch support, BoatFly. That's a serious, purpose-built anarchy and vanilla-survival toolkit, and on a server like 2b2t it's doing precisely the job it was built for. Combat modules exist too, but they're not the center of gravity.

Opal's module set points the other direction: a combat suite with its own targeting and aim-assist subpackages, a bridging assist module, movement tools, and a visual set built around fast-paced fights. The louder side of that set is tuned for BedWars and SkyWars-style minigame combat, and a quieter set is built to hold up on most other servers too. Neither client is trying to win the other's game.

If you want dupe finders and auto-builders on an anarchy server, Wurst is the tool for the job and Opal isn't in that conversation. If you're fighting in minigame lobbies, flip that sentence around.

## A hand-built menu vs a scripting engine and a UI runtime

Wurst's ClickGUI and Navigator are hand-built OpenGL drawn straight over Minecraft's own GuiGraphics. No embedded browser, no third-party UI library, nothing to load. That's a deliberate choice, it's small and predictable, and it's held up for a decade without needing to be rebuilt. If "read every line yourself" matters to you, that simplicity is worth something real.

Opal goes further down the stack: an embedded GraalJS scripting engine lets users load and unload JavaScript against the client's internal APIs while it's running, and the UI itself is a React-style layer hosted inside that same GraalJS runtime, laid out with Yoga flexbox over a custom Blaze3D effects pipeline. It's a heavier, more animated interface built for polish, and the cost of that is the plain, auditable transparency Wurst offers by default. Pick your trade-off.
