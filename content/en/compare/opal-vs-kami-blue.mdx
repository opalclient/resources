## Free and open source vs $8.99 and closed

KAMI Blue costs nothing, and its source is open. Read it, fork it, or build your own tools on top of it, all without paying anyone. That is a real advantage, and it is not one Opal offers. If open source and zero cost are non-negotiable for you, that is a genuinely strong reason to reach for KAMI Blue.

Opal costs $8.99, once. What that buys you is not the code, it is a finished product: a launcher, a polished UI, and a module set that is already tuned for minigame combat before you have touched a single settings panel. You are not paying for something KAMI Blue lacks. You are paying to skip building it yourself.

## Anarchy vs minigames: not really the same fight

KAMI Blue is an anarchy client, the kind of tool built for griefing and survival-anarchy servers where the whole point is chaos and self defense. Opal is built around BedWars and SkyWars-style minigame combat, with a quieter feature set meant to hold up on most other servers.

Putting these two head to head does not really work as a rivalry. If you live on anarchy servers, KAMI Blue is built for that world and Opal was never trying to be. If you play minigames, the reverse is true. Neither client loses this comparison so much as it was never really in it.

## Maintained vs legacy: version support

KAMI Blue is marked legacy, sitting on Minecraft 1.12.2 and an old Forge build, with no active updates for current Minecraft releases. Opal tracks MC 26.2, the current release, and is actively developed.

If you are on a modern client and want something that keeps pace with new Minecraft versions, KAMI Blue stopped being that project a while ago. If you are specifically on 1.12.2 because that is where your anarchy server lives, version currency stops being the question that matters.

## What KAMI Blue actually got right

KAMI Blue's codebase was solid enough that other MC 1.12.2 clients, SalHack among them, built on top of its base API instead of starting from scratch. That is a genuine mark of a well-built free client: other projects chose to extend it rather than replace it. Opal was never trying to be that kind of project. It is a paid product for players who want a finished client, not a foundation to build on, and there is no shade in saying so.
