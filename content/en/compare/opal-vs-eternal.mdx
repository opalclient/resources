## What we actually know about Eternal

Not much, and that's worth admitting instead of faking a fuller comparison. Eternal shows up in our records under a second name, Centauri, and as a listing on the intent.store marketplace. That's it: no public price, no feature list, no module count, no UI stack, and no supported Minecraft version attached to it anywhere we could verify. That thin a listing isn't evidence of anything shady, it just means the marketplace page is the only place with current answers, since a client sold through a storefront can change its price and feature set without that ever showing up anywhere else. If you're looking at Eternal, read its own listing before you pay.

## What $8.99 actually gets you with Opal

Opal costs $8.99. For that you get a Fabric mod for MC 26.2 with 70 modules split across combat, movement, visual, world, and utility, a UI built on NanoVG with a Blaze3D-based effects layer for blur, refraction, and shadow, and an embedded GraalJS scripting engine that lets you load and unload custom scripts against the client's own APIs at runtime. That last part is rarer than it sounds: most paid clients at this price point don't ship a real scripting host, let alone one built on a full polyglot runtime.

## Built for minigame combat, with a quieter set for everywhere else

Opal splits its module set in two. The loud side, flight, scaffold, aggressive combat aids, is tuned for BedWars and SkyWars-style minigame combat. The quiet side, subtle aim, reach, velocity adjustments, is built to hold up across most servers instead of being tuned for one specific place. You're not buying a client that does one thing; you're buying one that's upfront about which of its own features are loud and which aren't.
