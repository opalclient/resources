<KeyTakeaways>

- Statistics is an on-screen module that shows live gameplay numbers, kills, deaths, and playtime, updating in real time as you play.
- It only reads data the game already tracks, so it never touches combat, movement, or any other mechanic, and carries no anticheat risk.
- Seen in clients like Fan and Tenacity as one of several optional on-screen overlays.

</KeyTakeaways>

The Statistics module is an on-screen panel that displays live gameplay numbers, kills, deaths, playtime, while you're actually playing, instead of making you back out to a menu to check them. It updates the moment something happens: land a kill and the number ticks up before you've even sheathed your sword. Unlike most of what gets covered on this site, it doesn't touch combat, movement, or anything else that changes how the game plays. It's a display feature, full stop, closer in spirit to a scoreboard than to anything an anticheat would ever care about.

## How the Statistics module works

A Statistics panel reads values the game, or the client, is already keeping internally: kill count, death count, time played, and renders them in a small block that stays pinned to a corner of the screen during normal play. There's nothing special about the read. The data exists whether or not you're watching it, the module just puts it somewhere visible instead of leaving it buried in a menu you'd otherwise pause and click through to reach.

Because it only reads and displays, it doesn't hook into anything that affects gameplay. It's closer kin to a scoreboard or an FPS counter than to a module that changes what you can do. People reach for it for the same reason anyone likes watching a live scoreboard: it turns an abstract session into a number that moves. Chasing a kill streak or keeping a rough eye on how long you've been playing is easier when the count sits on screen instead of locked behind a stats page you have to stop and open.

## Is it safe to use

Yes, and there's not much of a case to make otherwise. Statistics doesn't automate anything, doesn't reveal information about other players, and doesn't change combat or movement outcomes in any way. It surfaces numbers that were already being tracked. It doesn't manufacture new ones or talk to the server any differently than vanilla play would.

That puts it in the same bucket as a coordinates HUD or an FPS overlay: a local convenience with zero server-side footprint. There's no packet for an anticheat to flag, no timing pattern to analyze, no impossible input to catch, because there's no input to catch in the first place. Banning for this would mean banning someone for looking at their own screen.
