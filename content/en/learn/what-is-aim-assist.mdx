Aim assist is a combat module that nudges your aim toward a nearby target using small, human-plausible corrections instead of snapping your crosshair onto it outright. It's built as a quieter alternative to a full aimbot: it adjusts an aim that's already close rather than taking over tracking from scratch.

## How aim assist works

Aim assist only steps in when your view is already close to a target. Look somewhere else entirely and it does nothing. Once you're roughly on target, it applies a series of small corrections that pull your view a little closer over several frames, rather than snapping the crosshair straight onto the entity in one jump.

The point of spreading the correction across frames is that each individual movement stays inside the range of a normal mouse turn. A snapping aimbot produces one sharp, obvious jump. Aim assist produces many small ones, and each looks plausible on its own, so a single frame or a short clip tends to read as ordinary tracking rather than an instant lock.

That also defines its ceiling. Because it only nudges an aim you've already roughly placed, it does nothing for a target you haven't noticed. It's an accuracy boost layered on top of your own tracking, not a replacement for it, which is the whole reason it reads as the lighter-touch option next to a full aimbot.

## Aim assist vs a full aimbot

The two sit on the same spectrum but land in different places on it.

| | Aim assist | Full aimbot |
|---|---|---|
| Starting point | Your view is already near the target | Your view can be anywhere |
| Correction style | Small pulls spread across several frames | One direct snap onto the target |
| Single-frame look | Reads as normal tracking | Reads as a sudden lock |
| What you still do | Most of the aiming | Little to none |

Because it needs a rough lock to begin with, aim assist reads as a boost to your own aim rather than a stand-in for it. That's also why it's the harder of the two to catch on any individual swing, and why the difference matters more to an anticheat looking at a pattern than to a spectator watching one clip.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Aim assist on Opal">
Opal's catalog lists Aim Assist plainly: it nudges your aim toward nearby targets with legit-style corrections. Same category, same honesty about what it is, no dressing it up as anything other than an aim tool.
</Callout>

<Callout variant="warning" title="Use at your own risk">
Aim assist is not a safe feature. A correction applied to your rotation is still a change to your aim, and a server watching aim data across many hits, not just one frame, can pick up a pattern of aim that consistently improves toward a target in a way manual play doesn't reproduce. The same small increments that make one correction hard to notice are, in aggregate, a pattern of their own. Treat a ban as a real possibility, not an edge case, on Opal or any other client.
</Callout>
