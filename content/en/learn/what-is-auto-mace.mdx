Auto Mace is a Minecraft combat module that automates the wind-charge-into-fall-swing combo behind the mace's bonus smash damage. Instead of the player judging height, timing a launch, and swinging at the exact moment of impact, the module runs the whole sequence itself: launch, track the fall, swing. It shows up in clients built around mace-era combat, Rise Nextgen among them, and it turns a combo that takes real practice into one that fires the same way on every attempt.

## How Auto Mace works

The mace deals bonus damage scaled to how far the attacker fell when the hit lands while they're still falling, a mechanic often called a smash attack. Getting that bonus normally takes two things done right: gaining height first, usually with a wind charge that launches the player upward, then swinging at the exact instant of landing on the target below. Miss the timing by a beat and the hit either whiffs or lands with less fall distance and less bonus damage to show for it.

That's the part Auto Mace removes. It triggers the wind-charge launch, tracks the resulting fall in real time, and fires the mace swing at the point in that fall that produces the largest smash-attack bonus, all without separate manual inputs from the player. A wind-charge mace combo done by hand takes practice: launching to the right height, staying above the target, hitting the exact frame of impact. Miss any part of it and you lose damage, sometimes the fight. Auto Mace turns that into a hit you can count on, close to maximum smash damage, without training the timing yourself.

## Is Auto Mace detectable

The mace and its fall-damage bonus are ordinary game mechanics, so a hit landed through Auto Mace isn't physically impossible the way a fabricated critical hit or extra reach would be. What gives it away is consistency, not physics. A player running the combo by hand varies in launch height, positioning, and swing timing, and regularly falls short of the maximum bonus. Auto Mace lands close to peak fall distance on nearly every attempt and chains the wind-charge input to the attack input with a precision no manual player sustains across a full fight.

That combination, automated input timing paired with unusually consistent damage output, is exactly what server-side anticheats and staff reviewing combat logs look for. It's also worth separating Auto Mace from something like Criticals: Criticals forces every hit to register as a critical even when the player isn't actually falling, which fakes a game state outright. Auto Mace doesn't fake anything. It automates real, legitimate inputs, wind charge then swing, with a consistency no human replicates. Most servers prohibit automating combat regardless, so the distinction matters for understanding the mechanic, not for justifying running it.
