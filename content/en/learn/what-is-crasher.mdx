- It gives no combat or movement advantage, only removing one opponent.
- It's documented in Tenacity, filed under exploits, not combat or movement cheats.

Crasher is a Minecraft exploit module that sends malformed or oversized packets at another player's client, aiming to crash it outright. It doesn't sharpen anyone's aim or speed anyone's movement. It just tries to end one specific person's session. That's a different animal from most things people mean when they say "cheat," and it's worth understanding why.

## How it works

Crasher doesn't touch the server. It builds a packet designed to break the target's client, usually malformed data or a payload the client's parser can't handle safely, and sends it straight at that one player. If their client fails to process it cleanly, it can hang, throw an error, or crash and take the whole game session down with it.

Because the attack exploits how the target's client reads incoming data rather than anything the server enforces, results are inconsistent. A client with strict packet validation rejects the garbage outright and nothing happens. A client without that hardening can go down instantly. Same module, wildly different outcome, depending entirely on what the person on the other end is running.

## Crasher vs. a combat or movement cheat

Most modules that get called "cheats" exist to make the person running them better: sharper aim, faster movement, more information than they'd normally have. Crasher does none of that for its own user. It aims outward, not inward.

| | Combat/movement cheat (KillAura, Reach) | Crasher |
|---|---|---|
| Who it helps | The user | No one |
| What it changes | The user's aim, range, or speed | The target's client stability |
| Effect on the target | None directly | Hang, error, or crash |
| Category | Combat / Movement | Exploit (griefing) |

That's why it's filed under exploits rather than sitting next to a module like [KillAura](/en/learn/what-is-killaura) or [Reach](/en/learn/what-is-reach). It's closer to a targeted denial-of-service trick aimed at one client than to anything resembling fair (or even unfair) play.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Treated as griefing, banned on sight">
Crasher isn't a subtle statistical edge an anticheat has to infer from behavior. A server can log the malformed packets directly, and any staff member reviewing a report sees a player's game freeze or close the moment it fires. Because the intent to disrupt someone's session is unmistakable, servers ban for it when reported, regardless of whether an anticheat ever flagged anything.
</Callout>
