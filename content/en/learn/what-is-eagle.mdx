Eagle is a Minecraft movement module that sneaks for you automatically, but only right at the edge of a block. It reproduces the safety of Ninja Bridge, the habit of holding shift while bridging so you don't walk off the edge before the next block lands, without making you actually hold the key. You walk and place blocks like normal. Eagle watches the ledge and handles the sneaking itself, engaging for the moment it matters and releasing the rest of the time.

## How it works

Eagle watches your position relative to the block underneath you on every tick. The moment you're close enough to an edge that a step forward would drop you, sneak engages on its own. As soon as you move back toward the center of the block, it releases and you're walking at full speed again.

From the outside this looks exactly like ordinary bridging. The difference is that you never hold shift yourself, so movement only slows during the instants sneaking is actually doing something, not for the length of the whole bridge. That's the specific gap Eagle is built for: manual sneak-bridging is safe but slow, and bridging without sneaking at all risks walking off the block before the next one lands. Eagle is documented running in clients like Juul and Rose, doing the same job wherever it shows up.

## Is it safe to use

Automatic, conditional sneaking isn't normal client behavior. Vanilla Minecraft doesn't toggle sneak on its own based on where you're standing, so a server watching movement has something concrete to compare against. Sneak input that fires exactly at block edges, every time, faster and more consistently than a human manages by hand, is the kind of pattern a server-side check can pick up on.

That doesn't put Eagle in the same league as flight or a speed hack. It's quieter, closer to the "looks like normal play from across the server" end of the spectrum. But quiet isn't invisible, and whether a given server catches it comes down to how closely that server's anticheat watches sneak timing.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Opal calls this Eagle too">
Opal ships the same module under the same name. It sneaks at block edges automatically, the Ninja Bridge technique, so the fall protection is there without babysitting the shift key. Opal classifies it use-at-own-risk, same as here: it's detectable, and running it carries the same ban risk as any client's version of the module.
</Callout>
