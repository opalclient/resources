- AntiAura is a defensive module that predicts an opponent's crystal, anchor, or totem timing and repositions the player clear of the blast.
- It calculates the blast timing the same way vanilla Minecraft resolves it.
- It changes only the player's own movement, never the opponent's client.
- It is documented in Tenacity, and most crystal-PvP bans cover it too.

</KeyTakeaways>

AntiAura is a defensive Minecraft module that predicts when an opponent's end crystal, respawn anchor, or totem attack is about to explode and moves the player out of the blast radius before it lands. It does not touch the enemy's client, place a block, or throw a punch. All it changes is the wearer's own movement, timed to a precision no set of human reflexes reaches on its own. Where a module like Crystal Aura automates the offense, AntiAura automates the read: notice the setup, calculate the blast, step clear.

## How AntiAura works

AntiAura watches for the exact setup each offensive Aura module relies on: an end crystal about to be struck, a charged respawn anchor about to be hit, or a totem-triggered attack about to fire. Once it spots that setup, it calculates the moment the explosion will land using the same timing math vanilla Minecraft already uses to resolve the blast. Nothing exotic, just the client running the numbers faster than a person can.

From there it repositions the player, stepping, strafing, or jumping, so they are standing outside the blast radius the instant the explosion actually happens. That is the key distinction: this is a prediction executed ahead of the hit, not a reaction to damage already taken. It is documented as seen in Tenacity, where it lives in the exploit category next to other modules that read and act on opponent state instead of the player's own inputs.

## AntiAura vs the modules it counters

AntiAura exists because of modules like [Crystal Aura](/en/learn/what-is-a-crystal-aura) and Anchor Aura, which automatically place and detonate an explosive next to a target. A player without any assistance has to spot the crystal or anchor, judge the timing, and move before it goes off, all inside a fraction of a second. AntiAura automates that read-and-dodge sequence the same way the offensive modules automate the place-and-detonate sequence on the other side of the fight.

The two are not identical in what they touch, though. An offensive Aura module sends placement and interaction packets a server can inspect directly, block by block. AntiAura only changes movement, the same category of input the client is already sending every tick, which is exactly what makes its output blend in with ordinary play at a glance.

## Is it safe to use

Mostly no, even though the risk profile looks different from an offensive Aura module. AntiAura does not place blocks, does not attack, and does not exceed any vanilla movement limit on its own, so it lacks the obvious tells, impossible reach, interactions with a block the player never faced, that make something like Crystal Aura easy to flag from server logs alone.

What it still automates is a dodge that lands with a consistency and reaction speed no human matches by eye. Servers running anticheats built around crystal or anchor PvP tend to treat that kind of tick-perfect evasive movement as a gameplay advantage rather than a lucky coincidence, especially once it repeats across a full match's worth of explosions. Catching it takes movement analysis instead of a simple packet check, but on servers that ban automated combat assistance in general, it falls under the same rule as the modules it is dodging.
