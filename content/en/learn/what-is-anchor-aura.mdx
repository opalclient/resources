Anchor Aura is a combat module that automatically places a respawn anchor next to a target and detonates it, turning a block most players only ever see in the Nether into a PvP weapon anywhere in the world. The player doesn't place the anchor, doesn't charge it, and doesn't swing at it. The module runs the entire sequence itself, faster than a hand ever could.

## How it works

Anchor Aura runs a place-and-detonate loop against a nearby target, and the loop is a near-copy of what a [Crystal Aura](/en/learn/what-is-a-crystal-aura) does with end crystals. It picks a target, scans for a valid spot to drop a respawn anchor next to that target, places the anchor, and then triggers it so the blast lands on the enemy.

The mechanic underneath all of this is vanilla: a charged respawn anchor explodes if it's struck outside the Nether. Normally that means placing the block, filling it with glowstone charges, and hitting it at the right moment, three separate actions a person has to land in sequence. Anchor Aura collapses all three into one automated step, and it does it more consistently than someone managing the inputs by hand ever will.

You'll find it in Wurst and Meteor Client, where it lives in the combat category next to the other automated explosive modules.

## Anchor Aura vs Crystal Aura

Strip away the block name and these are the same module. Both auto-place an explosive beside a target and detonate it on a loop with zero manual input. The only real difference is the explosive itself. A [Crystal Aura](/en/learn/what-is-a-crystal-aura) uses end crystals, which can be placed on obsidian or bedrock almost anywhere on the map. Anchor Aura is stuck with a Nether-specific rule: the anchor has to be struck outside the Nether to go off, which is exactly the loophole the module exploits to weaponize it in the Overworld or the End.

Pick whichever block is available and the underlying automation doesn't care. Anticheats treat them the same way too.

## Why it gets detected

Anchor Aura is a gameplay-advantage cheat, not a cosmetic one, so servers police it the same way they police a Crystal Aura or any other auto-combat module. The place-and-detonate timing runs faster than manual play allows, and the anchor frequently goes down at a spot the player's camera never actually pointed at.

Server-side anticheats are built to catch exactly that shape of pattern: blocks placed and triggered in rapid succession near an entity, interactions with no matching player rotation, and explosion damage that lines up with a loop instead of a human sequence of place, charge, and strike. Trip enough of those and the account gets flagged for review or banned outright, depending on the server.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Where Opal draws the line">
Opal doesn't ship a dedicated Anchor Aura. Auto-explosive loops like this are about as loud as combat automation gets: the timing signature and off-angle placements are the first thing a server-side anticheat looks for, which makes the module a fast way to lose an account. Opal's combat modules exist, but they're built around staying inside what a server can't distinguish from a human, not around detonating blocks nobody aimed at.
</Callout>
