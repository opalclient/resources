<KeyTakeaways>
- MCF is a module in the Tenacity Minecraft client's misc/other category, with no documented function anywhere in the public record.
- No source explains what the letters stand for or what changes when a player toggles it on.
- It sits alongside modules like AntiFreeze, AutoRespawn, HackerDetector, Sniper, and Spammer in Tenacity's module list.
- Nothing confirms MCF is harmless, and nothing confirms it's bannable. Treat it as unverified, not safe by default.
</KeyTakeaways>

MCF is a module that shows up in the misc/other section of the Tenacity Minecraft client's module list, and that's close to the entire public record on it. No changelog entry, wiki line, or settings screenshot explains what the three letters stand for or what actually changes when you flip the toggle. That's an unusual amount of silence for something sitting in a client menu next to modules like AntiFreeze and AutoRespawn, both of which do exactly what their names promise. MCF promises nothing, because nobody wrote down what it does.

## What's actually documented about MCF

The full trail on MCF is three things: its name, its category, and the client it appeared in. There's no setting list, no in-game description, no forum thread walking through behavior, no clip demonstrating it. That's the entirety of the record, and it has stayed that way.

Tenacity's misc/other category is a mixed bag by design. AntiFreeze and AutoRespawn are quality-of-life toggles that do what they say on the label. HackerDetector flags other players. Sniper and Spammer lean toward server interaction and combat-adjacent behavior. Sitting in that same grouping means MCF could plausibly be any of those flavors, and the placement alone tells you nothing about which one it is.

Short, unlabeled abbreviations like this aren't rare in clients that have passed through several maintainers. The explanation usually lived in a commit message or a Discord thread that never made it into a changelog, and once active development on a client slows down, the meaning gets lost while the compiled toggle stays exactly where it was.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Documentation isn't optional">
This is the gap a proper module list is supposed to close. Opal documents every module it ships, across combat, movement, visual, and world categories, with a real description attached to the module and to every setting inside it. If a toggle exists, there's a sentence next to it explaining what it changes. No digging through old commits required.
</Callout>

## Is it safe to use

There's no honest way to answer that yes or no. Nothing in the record confirms MCF is a harmless cosmetic toggle, and nothing confirms it touches gameplay or the server connection in a way an anticheat would care about. Assuming either extreme is unsupported by what's actually documented.

The honest position is that MCF is undocumented, not disproven. Turning it on means enabling something you can't fully account for, which is a meaningfully different risk than a module with a known description you're simply choosing not to read.

<Callout variant="warning" title="An unlabeled toggle is still a toggle">
Enabling a module you can't explain is a bet, not a shortcut. If MCF touches server-side behavior in any way, an anticheat won't care that nobody documented it first. Test undocumented toggles in single-player or on a server you don't mind losing, never on an account or a server that actually matters to you.
</Callout>
