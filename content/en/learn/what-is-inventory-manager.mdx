Inventory Manager is a module in Minecraft utility clients that automatically sorts and stacks your inventory so matching items end up grouped together instead of scattered across random slots. It's the same result you'd get from manually dragging every half-stack of cobblestone or scrap of food into place, just without the tedium. Trigger it once and the mess sorts itself. It doesn't add loot, delete anything, or reach into chests or other containers. It only rearranges what's already sitting in your own inventory, which is exactly why it's filed under convenience rather than combat or movement.

## How it works

Inventory Manager scans your inventory and rearranges the contents so identical items combine into full stacks and similar categories, tools, blocks, food, end up sitting near each other. Run it and you get the layout you'd reach after a few minutes of manual sorting, delivered in an instant instead.

The module stays deliberately narrow in scope. It only touches placement within the inventory screen you already control. Nothing gets spawned, nothing gets deleted, and it doesn't interact with chests, crafting, or anything else outside your own inventory tab. It moves items you already have to slots they already could have gone to by hand.

## Why bother automating it

Inventories fill with partial stacks and scattered item types during mining, building, or looting, and straightening them out one drag at a time gets old fast. A sorted inventory is easier to read at a glance, which matters most in the moment you actually need something, a specific tool or a food source buried under a dozen other slots.

Because it only reorganizes what's already there, Inventory Manager doesn't touch drop rates, combat outcomes, or anything else a server keeps score of. It's closer to a UI convenience than a gameplay modifier, sitting alongside modules like auto-armor equipping and auto-tool selection rather than anything combat or movement related.

## Is it safe to use

Yes, and it's not a close call. Inventory Manager only rearranges the contents of your own inventory, the same action a player can already perform by hand one slot at a time. Servers don't treat inventory order as meaningful data, so there's simply nothing for an anticheat to observe or flag. Opal classifies it as a safe module for exactly this reason: it's cosmetic and quality-of-life, the same bucket as auto-armor and other organizational tools, well away from where detection risk actually concentrates.
